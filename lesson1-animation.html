Introduction to an Animator's Fundamental Toolkit

Traditional animation is the foundation for all other types of animation that exist today.

Traditional animation is a technique in which each frame is created by hand and is frequently referred to as cel animation (cel is short for celluloid, which is a translucent sheet on which things are drawn). It is sometimes referred to as classical or hand-drawn animation. You'll need a few basic supplies in your workplace or home if you want to work on cel-painted animation. The fundamentals cover everything from your initial ideas to the painted cells required to bring the animation to life.

More Information

1. Animation disc - Is a drawing disc placed on a light box and used to work out camera moves and panning walk cycles.

2. Light box - used to trace images or patterns from one source to another.

3. Pencil and eraser – Used to rough out first key drawings

4. Peg bar - Peg bars are normally found at the bottom and top of animation discs. Before digital compositing, the peg bars were also used to move layers on the camera stand to create pans and camera moves.

5. Brush - a device composed of bristles typically set into a handle and used especially for sweeping, smoothing, scrubbing, or painting

6. Animation Paper - Animation Paper will have exactly what you need as an animator. No more, no less. Even if you are working in 3D, stop motion or cinematography, Animation Paper will be of great value for quickly testing your ideas, rough out key poses and frames - and check your timing possibilities.

7. Printable Storyboard - A storyboard template is a graphic organizer that presents a story narrative. Students can use a storyboard template to visually present story details and the sequence of events.

8. Exposure sheet - is a traditional animation tool that allows an animator to organize his thinking and give instructions to the cameraman on how the animation is to be shot.

9. Scanner – used an alternative device to camera

10. Camera Rig - a piece of filming equipment to which you can attach your camera, and it aids in the filming process.

Facts

The Animator's Toolkit has counterparts for digital animators too:

1. Adobe After Effects - Digital artists can use this commercial tool to add visual effects and motion graphics elements to film, TV, video, online content, presentations, and more.

2. Adobe Animate - Users can create animations across multiple media, from cartoons and banner ads to interactive content for games, TV shows, academic content, and infographics.

3. Blender – This free open source 3D graphics creation suite offers a comprehensive feature set to create animations, digital art, visual effects, 2D and 3D models, and more.

4. OpenToonz - This software tool allows digital artists to create 2D animations. As open source software, it’s free to use, anyone can modify its source code, and it’s frequently updated.

5. Stop Motion Studio – This software provides an intuitive interface. Its features include frame-by-frame editing to allow digital artists of all ages to create stop motion movies using a desktop or phone app.

6. PowToon - This cloud-based animation software for digital artists and amateurs alike in business, education, and other sectors allows users to create animated explainer videos, demos, and presentations.
